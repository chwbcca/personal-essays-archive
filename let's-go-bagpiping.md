# Let's Go Bagpiping!
**Original Date:** May 24, 2012

Let's go bagpiping! It is always the same old story when I tell it—about how a young Mexican Jewish boy could wear a kilt and go around making lots of noise. How come? What made you do it? Was it difficult? If you are looking for the answers to those questions, then you should look somewhere else.

But bagpiping in Mexico was definitely quite an experience, and it was through bagpiping there that many doors were opened to places and people that I never even knew existed. Many of these people and places shaped the way I go through life these days, including my insatiable thirst for a cold Guinness on a warm day.

I began playing the bagpipes around the time I turned 14. To be honest, I was never a star or anything close to it, and it was only through hard work, dedication, and a bit of luck that I was able to join the one and only pipe band in Mexico: the St. Patrick’s Battalion Pipe Band. It was affectionately named after a historical event in Mexico’s past when a bunch of crazy Irishmen and other Europeans defected and tried to defend Mexico against the United States, and instead got hanged. It sounds like a big band, but I think at the height of my best years there, we must have had no more than 8 bagpipe players, 6 drummers, and a pipe major. So all in all, not such a big band, but we could surely make quite a racket. On the plus side, we also had a partnership with an Irish dance squad, which was nice because we sometimes had people dancing to the tune of our bagpipes. It was also a nice thing because some of the dancers there were quite fit and friendly, and being 15 and having pretty girls around was pretty awesome.

But this is not about numbers, or dancers, or puberty. None of that. Instead, just last night I was reminded of my trial by fire with bagpipes, which began a year or so after I had begun learning the instrument.

Contrary to popular belief, the bagpipes are learned first on a smaller flute called a practice chanter, which sounds very shrill and emulates some of the fingerwork that the bagpipes require. The bagpipes themselves are about 300% louder than said chanter and a lot more difficult to play, just because there are more moving parts. It is important to keep all these moving parts in full working order to avoid sounding like a swarm of bees or, worse, a squad of cats getting slaughtered.

After a year of playing the chanter, my mother was gracious and smart enough to buy me a set of bagpipes, which were not cheap at all. Even the cheapest sets made from plastic range around the $700 mark, and for a 15-year-old kid, making that much money in Mexico can be a very daunting task. My mother helped me out and made my dream happen. Of course, we did not settle for the cheap plastic set and instead opted for a $1,200 behemoth set made with real African Blackwood, stainless steel accents, and silk adornments. It was a really swell set that was beautiful to look at but, well, pretty damn hard to play.

Moving on to the bagpipes from playing the chanter can be a real challenge because the bagpipes have at least four important parts: the blowpipe (where you blow air), the airbag (where the air goes and which you have to squeeze to keep the pressure steady), the drones (which are three massive, towering sticks that hang on your shoulder, emit a low hum, and are a hassle to tune), and finally, the chanter itself, which is where, if you are lucky, you play the musical notes and they sound well. Keeping all of these parts in full working order can sometimes be a very daunting task, but my teacher at the time—a charismatic Irishman by the name of Michael Kelly—was confident I could do it, and that I could do it right in front of a live audience.

His idea of a trial by fire was something I might never forget. I must have been around sixteen years of age or so at the time. It was a Saturday morning almost like any other, except that I was a little bit hungover from drinking too much on a Friday night with some friends of mine. It was not pretty. Being underage in Mexico, we could get any alcohol we wanted, but we had no idea how to mix or match, so our idea of a good cocktail was to mix rum and vodka, add ice, and drink until you forgot your name.

Around 9:00 AM, my charismatic teacher called me and asked if I would like to do a small demonstration in an orphanage—just a quick gig, he said. I asked for more details and he said he had met a very nice woman who ran an orphanage for girls a little ways outside of Mexico City, and she had invited him to do a small demonstration for the girls in order to bring some sort of new culture and spark some curiosity into their lives. I was a little bit afraid because when I would play the bagpipes, it was normally just at band practice or during shy demonstrations for family and friends. It was always a bit messy because playing the bagpipes is a matter of open-air chaos, not really a small-room instrument. I would brag that indoors, the bagpipes are so loud they can bring people back from the dead. I never really had the guts to go out into the streets and play on my own, or go around with my bagpipes, as their intensity and their cost made me wary of taking them to too many places. And so, after a bit of persuasion on his part, I agreed to go and join him on this Saturday adventure to God knows where.

We were picked up by the lady’s driver and driven to our location in a nice, big sedan; I think it was a Nissan. The lady who ran the orphanage was in the car with us and was chatting about everything and nothing, but then again, I was so nervous I couldn't really remember what we talked about. The orphanage was outside of the city, about an hour away, and was a unique orphanage because Korean nuns ran it. Huh?!?!

At the time, I had no idea there were any Koreans in Mexico. At that same time, I had no idea there were any Koreans who were Christian. At that same time, I had no idea there were Korean nuns! So indeed, I was a little bit confused. That made my ride there even a bit more difficult, and I was even more nervous.

After two hours or so of driving, we arrived, fully dressed in kilts, to a massive compound that housed more than 3,000 orphan girls who had come from all over Mexico to live better lives and receive a good education there. They were all fed three square meals a day and taught a curriculum full of normal academics and courses that could help them find jobs once they left the orphanage. Many of them were taught sewing, painting, woodworking, etc. In a sense, it had everything an orphan needed to succeed in life, and now, they had a couple of crazy bagpipers as a bonus.

To my surprise, my teacher had not told me how many people would be attending our small demonstration, and from his original account, I thought maybe there would be a few dozen girls watching us perform. But oh no, it was not to be so simple. We were escorted to a massive auditorium, which was also their gym. A stage had been set up and chairs had been lined up to see us perform. Inside this massive gym, there were 3,500 girls waiting to see us perform! I could not believe my eyes—3,500 girls! And also, sadly, I could not believe my nose, because the gym was not air-conditioned and these girls were not provided with deodorant, so the smell of the gym that day was the first thing that hit me. I was not angry or taken aback by this; I was just amazed at how pungent that place was.

After a very brief introduction as to who we were and what our names were, we launched into a 25-minute medley set that seemed to last for a lifetime, at least for me. After we played each of the different tunes in the medley, we would stop and explain the melodies and their functions in the music world. Of course, being that my teacher was an Irishman and I was the only Mexican next to him who spoke English, it was not only my task to play the bagpipes in front of all these girls, but also to translate for my teacher and be our MC. His charismatic jokes in English did not translate too well into Spanish, by the way. This was the first time I had ever spoken in front of so many people, and I was glad that I was wearing a kilt and sweating profusely under a suit and tie; otherwise, I would have been making a fool of myself in normal attire. It was a little bit nerve-wracking to do those two seemingly simple things in front of everyone, but after we finished playing, I was glad it was over.

Everyone applauded, and we were both given a small bouquet of flowers as a gift for our appearance. Then, some sweet old nuns invited us to have lunch with them. We walked around the compound some more and reached their home, where we were served freshly slaughtered chicken, which I could barely eat because my hands were still shaking so much from nerves. Add to that that I was a very picky eater then, and the chicken we were given was so full of bones and cartilage that I could barely swallow a bite or two without gagging. I was so ashamed that I could not eat their chicken, and I was ashamed that I had no way of hiding this fact, but the nuns did not seem to mind too much. We finished our meal and were shortly after driven home.

On the drive home, I was a bit angry with my teacher because I was not ready to play the bagpipes in front of so many people. I was not ready to wear a kilt in front of 3,500 young girls who, I was told later, were all my age—about 16 or so. Thank God I was wearing underwear.

I was a bit angry because I was so ignorant of so many things and had just turned up to realize this. I was glad that we played well and that the girls and the nuns seemed to enjoy our show. After all, what else were we supposed to do? I really hope that the girls enjoyed our show then and did not mind my nerves and my being so green, but after that day, I truly felt I could overcome anything. Maybe the next time I was offered chicken that was difficult to chew, I would eat it with confidence and a lot less gagging. And I made sure to improve my public speaking skills and my bagpiping as well.
